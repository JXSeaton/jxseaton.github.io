Project Overview

NECSUS is an advanced learning technology designed to test whether student engagement can be increased without teacher presence in online education.

The Challenge

GSR 960, a graduate ethics course at the University of Saskatchewan, was designed to be taken online with minimal student-teacher interaction. The course is short, pass/fail, and text-based.

The research question: Can we build a system that encourages student engagement and high-order thinking without the presence of an instructor?

Key Innovations

  • Game-inspired friendship mechanics
  • Test challenge feature enabling peer assessment
  • Social network visualization for course navigation
  • Strategic interdependence through collaborative challenges

Research Context

Examining the Community of Inquiry framework and its limitations in online learning environments

Community of Inquiry Model

The conceptual framework argues that community supports higher-order thinking through three presences:

  • Teacher Presence: Structuring and facilitating discourse
  • Social Presence: Projection of personal characteristics
  • Cognitive Presence: Construction of meaning through communication

Some researchers argue that without teacher presence, students will not engage in substantial discourse.

The Research Gap

If the Community of Inquiry model were comprehensive, we would expect:

  • No signs of Community of Inquiry in a course without instructor presence
  • No evidence of higher-order thinking
  • Limited student engagement and discussion

NECSUS aimed to test these assumptions through gamified social mechanics.

Design Process

An iterative approach to designing and refining game-inspired mechanics for educational engagement

Initial Concept

Inspired by game dynamics found in The Sims, EverQuest, and Farmville which all explicitly require strategic friendships.

First Prototype

Developed core features: friendship system, navigation map, and test challenge feature. Tested with 13 graduate students in Fall 2012.

Key Finding

Sparse usage meant students weren't in close enough temporal proximity to interact. The test challenge feature was the most successful aspect.

Redesign

Added "Practice Area" with ethical questions, implemented a scoring system and Kobayashi Maru challenge, and enhanced social presence elements.

Final Testing

Tested with two groups of students (26 total) in Winter 2013 to measure improved engagement and higher-order thinking.

Strategic Social Relationships

Inspired by The Sims, the friendship system required social grooming to maintain connections:

  • Students began with full friendship with all other students (900 points)
  • Friendship points deteriorated over time (3 points per hour)
  • Social interactions (replies, likes, profile visits) added approximately two days to the friendship
  • When friendship fell below threshold (500 points), friends disappeared from the map
  • Students could only see forum posts from people they were friends with

This system encouraged ongoing engagement and reciprocal interaction between students.

Social Navigation Map

A visual representation of the course content with an overlay of the student's social network:

  • Course content displayed as interconnected nodes
  • Friends appeared on the map, indicating the social nature of learning
  • Health points system affected map visibility (fading when inactive)
  • Discussion links indicated number of active threads
  • Visual feedback encouraged regular participation

This design highlighted the social aspects of the course, unlike traditional LMS platforms that aren't designed to support social awareness.

Test Challenge Feature

A mechanism that created interdependence among students and enabled peer assessment:

  • Students could challenge multiple-choice answers marked wrong
  • Challenges required creating a forum post explaining their reasoning
  • If three other students supported the argument (by liking), the challenge was won
  • Only friends could see challenge posts, requiring network maintenance
  • Encouraged higher-order thinking and peer evaluation

This feature successfully demonstrated students could assess each other's understanding without instructor intervention.

Practice Area

Added in the second iteration to model desired discussion behavior:

  • Presented ambiguous ethical questions based on course content
  • Asked students how they would resolve ethical conflicts
  • Two questions automatically posted each week
  • Scoring system awarded 10 points when suggestions received likes
  • Students could trade 30 points for a Kobayashi Maru Challenge

This feature simulated aspects of teacher presence by providing discussion models and encouraging exploration of course concepts.

Research Findings

Evidence of higher-order thinking and social presence without instructor interaction

Community of Inquiry Indicators

Key Insights

Despite the absence of an instructor, NECSUS successfully fostered:

  • Evidence of higher-order thinking (Integration and Resolution phases)
  • Social presence through multiple channels (posts, likes, profile customization)
  • Elements of teacher presence through system design

The disparity between cohorts suggested additional factors beyond teacher presence affect student engagement.

Student Engagement

Alternative engagement methods emerged:

  • Profile customization (6 students in Course 1, 7 in Course 2)
  • "Like" feature used by non-posting students
  • Profile walls used for social grooming
  • Test challenges as an outlet to demonstrate learning

Example: Peer Evaluation

Despite controlling for teacher presence, the two cohorts showed dramatically different engagement patterns, suggesting student agency plays a critical role in online educational communities.

Impact

NECSUS highlighted the importance of student agency in online learning environments. Given the exact same interface, different groups of students had distinct behavioural patterns. However, it was demonstrated that game dynamics can be utilized to elicit targeted behaviour from students. This indicates that gamification can be leveraged to increase student presence in lieu of teacher presence.

Further Work

This design was applied to a online snowmobile saftey course to test whether this solution could be adapted to other online learning environments that lack an instructor supervisor.
