Project Overview

This study investigates the experiences and engagement levels of online instructors at the University of Saskatchewan. Through a series of interviews conducted over a year, the research aims to understand the factors influencing instructors' participation and satisfaction in online teaching environments.

The Challenge

This project explores how online instructors experience engagement in the context of teaching at a research-intensive university. The study centers on the University of Saskatchewan and uses a case study approach to examine how institutional culture, workload, and personal motivation shape instructors’ participation in online teaching.

The research question: How do online instructors experience and describe engagement in their teaching practice within a research-intensive university setting?

Over the course of one year, twelve instructors were interviewed three times each to capture a longitudinal view of their teaching engagement. The study used a mixed-methods analysis that combined qualitative themes with sentiment scoring to assess the emotional tone of their reflections.

Key Findings

  • Institutional emphasis on research often overshadowed teaching responsibilities, leading to feelings of disconnection or decreased motivation among some instructors.
  • Instructors who identified more strongly with teaching as a professional identity tended to demonstrate higher and more consistent engagement.
  • Personal strategies for managing time and stress—such as scheduling boundaries and self-reflection—played a critical role in maintaining engagement.
  • The tone of instructors’ reflections shifted over time, with those experiencing increased institutional support or improved work-life balance showing a more positive sentiment by the final interview.

Overall, this project highlights the complex and evolving nature of engagement in online instruction, particularly in environments where research output is prioritized. It emphasizes the need for institutions to foster a culture that values and supports teaching to ensure instructor well-being and student success.

Research Context

Instructor Engagement is Underexplored

  • Most online education research focuses on students, technology, or course design.
  • There’s limited research on how instructors experience online teaching, especially their emotional and professional engagement.
  • Instructor engagement is often assumed rather than deeply examined.

Engagement is Contextual and Shaped by Institutions

  • Instructor engagement isn’t static. It shifts based on workload, identity, and institutional culture.
  • At research-intensive universities, teaching is often secondary to research in performance evaluations.
  • This dynamic can lead to reduced motivation and participation in online courses.

Lack of Understanding of Instructor Perspectives

  • While instructor presence and enthusiasm are known to affect student success, few studies at the point of this research centred on instructors' own voices.
  • There was a gap in understanding how instructors define, interpret, and sustain engagement over time.
  • Institutional support (or lack thereof) plays a crucial role in these experiences.

Addressing a Research and Practice Gap

  • This study gives instructors a platform to share their lived experiences.
  • It responds to the tension between research and teaching in academia.
  • The goal is to shift the conversation toward a more human-centered view of online education.

Research Design Process

Case Study Design

Focused on a single institution to explore instructors’ engagement in depth and over time. The qualitative case study approach allowed for rich, contextual understanding.

Participant Interviews

Twelve online instructors were interviewed three times over the span of one year. This longitudinal approach captured how experiences and engagement shifted over time.

Mixed Methods Analysis

Combined thematic coding with a unique sentiment scoring approach. Instructor responses were categorized based on whether their language conveyed positive or negative engagement.

Sentiment Tracking

Language was analyzed over time to quantify emotional tone. This created a numeric layer to the qualitative data, revealing patterns in how instructors’ attitudes changed.

Triangulation & Validation

Ensured trustworthiness through subject debriefing, research theme validation, and ongoing reflection throughout the research process.

Key Research Findings

Institutional Priorities Shape Engagement

  • Instructors perceived a strong institutional emphasis on research over teaching, which often led to lower engagement in online course design and delivery.
  • Some felt teaching was undervalued, especially in tenure and promotion discussions.
  • This tension created emotional strain for instructors who cared deeply about their teaching.

Teaching Identity Matters

  • Instructors who identified strongly as educators first (rather than primarily researchers) reported higher levels of engagement with online teaching tasks.
  • These instructors found it easier to invest time in course design and student interaction.
  • Teaching-oriented faculty expressed more positive engagement in their work.

Time and Workload Management are Critical

  • Competing demands between research, teaching, and personal responsibilities were a major source of disengagement.
  • Instructors who developed structured routines and boundaries were better able to stay engaged.
  • Those lacking time-management strategies often described burnout or detachment from their courses.

Emotional Tone Reflects Engagement

  • The sentiment analysis revealed clear emotional patterns:
    • More positive language often aligned with feelings of autonomy, support, and purpose.
    • More negative language reflected frustration, fatigue, and misalignment with institutional values.
  • These emotional shifts provided a unique, data-driven view of how instructors felt across time.

Impact

This study provides a nuanced, instructor-centered perspective on engagement in online teaching—an area often overlooked in digital education research. By foregrounding the voices of instructors and analyzing their emotional tone over time, the research brings much-needed attention to the human side of online education.

Key contributions include:

  • Highlighting how institutional culture and workload policies can enable or inhibit instructor engagement.
  • Demonstrating that teaching identity plays a significant role in online teaching satisfaction and motivation.
  • Offering a novel methodological approach that combines thematic analysis with sentiment tracking to reveal hidden patterns in instructor experience.

Ultimately, this work encourages universities to reevaluate how they support online instructors, particularly in environments where research is prioritized over teaching. The findings advocate for institutional policies that recognize and reward meaningful teaching engagement, especially in digital formats where faculty can feel isolated or undervalued.

Further Work

Building on this case study, the next phase of the research involves expanding the investigation to a broader institutional context. Specifically:

  • A survey-based study will be conducted at a second research-intensive university to examine whether the themes identified in this case study appear across different academic environments.
  • The survey will explore similar variables—teaching identity, institutional culture, time management, and emotional tone—to assess the transferability of the findings.
  • This comparative work is intended to strengthen the generalizability of the original findings and inform broader recommendations for supporting online instructors.

Limitations

While this research offers valuable insights, several limitations should be acknowledged:

  • The study focuses on a single institution, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other academic settings.
  • The sample size (12 instructors) is small, though typical for in-depth qualitative research.
  • Emotional sentiment was assessed using researcher-coded valence, which, while systematic, involves interpretive judgment and may carry some subjectivity.

Despite these limitations, the study provides a strong foundation for future research and institutional reflection on online teaching practices.
